Hamlet, Prince of Denmark, is arguably the most famous tragedy in the English language. It is set in the somber royal court of Elsinore, where the ghost of the recently deceased King Hamlet appears to his son, the protagonist. The Ghost reveals that he was brutally murdered in his sleep by his own brother, Claudius, who then quickly seized the throne and married the late king's wife, Queen Gertrude. This act is the core catalyst for the play's action, charging Hamlet with the duty of revenge.Faced with this monumental, supernatural command, Hamlet struggles with profound existential and moral dilemmas. His famous soliloquy, "To be, or not to be, that is the question," encapsulates his contemplation of suicide as a release from the suffering ("the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune") and his fear of the unknown that lies beyond death. To buy time and ascertain Claudius's guilt without revealing his hand, Hamlet adopts an "antic disposition" (feigned madness). This behavior isolates him, particularly from Ophelia, the woman he loves, who becomes deeply distressed by his erratic cruelty.Hamlet orchestrates a dramatic performance, "The Mousetrap" (a play-within-a-play), which mimics the precise manner of the old king's murder. Claudius's immediate, guilt-ridden exit from the audience confirms his culpability for Hamlet. This confirmation leads to a disastrous confrontation between Hamlet and his mother, Gertrude, during which Hamlet accidentally kills the listening courtier, Polonius, mistaking him for Claudius behind the tapestry.The final act is a cascade of tragic consequences: Ophelia, driven mad by grief and loss, drowns. Her brother, Laertes, returns seeking revenge for both his father's and sister's deaths. Claudius, ever the schemer, plots with Laertes to ensure Hamlet's demise in a duel. Laertes uses a poisoned rapier, and Claudius prepares a poisoned drink. In the chaotic final scene, Gertrude mistakenly drinks the poison, Laertes wounds Hamlet with the poisoned blade, Hamlet successfully wounds Laertes, and then, using the same poisoned sword, finally kills Claudius. As he lies dying, Hamlet passes the mantle of leadership to Fortinbras, the Prince of Norway, who arrives to survey the carnage and bring order to the corrupted state of Denmark. The play is a powerful examination of the paralyzing nature of thought in the face of action, the decay of political and familial bonds, and the pervasive nature of death.
